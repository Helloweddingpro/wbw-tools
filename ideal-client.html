What is an Ideal Client Avatar — and why does it matter?
Before we talk about who your most profitable client is, let's make sure you understand what an ICA actually is — and why most wedding suppliers get this completely wrong.
The Concept
An ICA is a detailed picture of one specific person

An Ideal Client Avatar — ICA for short — is a detailed description of the one type of client you most want to work with. Not a vague category like "couples with a budget over €2k." A real, specific person with a name, a lifestyle, values, habits, fears and dreams.

When you know exactly who she is, everything in your business gets easier. Your content speaks directly to her. Your pricing reflects what she's willing to invest. Your enquiry responses feel personal, not generic. She feels like you understand her before you've even met.

"When you try to speak to everyone, you speak to no one. The more specific you are about who you want to attract, the more magnetic your marketing becomes."

The Mistake Most Suppliers Make
They describe their current clients — not their dream ones

Here's the trap: when wedding suppliers are asked to describe their ideal client, they describe the couples they're already getting. If you're currently attracting mid-range bookings, you'll describe a mid-range client. And then your marketing keeps attracting more of the same.

This tool works differently. We're going to show you who your most profitable client is — based on real market insight — and then help you understand her well enough to start attracting her, even if she's not who you're working with right now.

What You'll Have By The End
A clear picture of your most profitable client — and how to reach her

By the time you finish this lesson you'll know exactly who your most profitable wedding client is, what she values, where she spends her time, what she's afraid of, and what makes her book. You'll also have a personalised version of her profile saved to your account — ready to connect directly with your Social Media Planner.

The insight that changes everything
There is one shift in thinking that can multiply what you earn — without changing what you do or how you do it. It has nothing to do with talent.
The Big Insight
Your most profitable client is already choosing her venue

Career couples — working professionals with strong household incomes — don't shop for suppliers the same way budget-conscious couples do. They don't spend weeks comparing prices on Google. They book their dream venue first, then build their supplier list around it.

This means the venue is your shortcut. When you become known at the top venues in your area, the right clients find you naturally — because those are the only weddings they're planning.

Why This Works
Top venues attract a specific type of couple

Couples booking at 4 and 5-star country house hotels and estate venues are not the same as couples booking a local hotel. They have higher budgets, value quality over bargain, and almost never haggle. They've already decided to invest in the best — they just need to know you're the right supplier for their venue.

When they search for a supplier, they often search by venue. "Wedding videographer Faithlegg." "Content creator Mount Juliet." If you're not showing up in those searches, you're invisible to your most profitable client.

"You don't need to find better clients. You need to show up where they already are. The venue is the filter — it does the qualifying for you."

Chapter 4 of 6
What this means for your marketing
Now that you know who Amy is, here's exactly how to put her in front of your content — and your content in front of her.
Insight 1
Make your venues the centre of your content

Amy searches by venue. Once she books Faithlegg House or Mount Juliet, she immediately looks for suppliers who have worked there before. She wants someone who knows the light, the layout, the team, the hidden spots. Post content from your top venues consistently. Tag the venues. Use their names in your captions.

This also builds your relationship with venue coordinators. When you show up consistently tagging their venue, they notice — and they start recommending you to couples who enquire directly with them.

Insight 2
Speak to her emotions, not her spreadsheet

Amy isn't comparing your package features line by line. She's imagining her wedding day and asking: does this person get it? Your captions, your website copy, your enquiry responses — they should describe feelings and moments, not megapixels and durations.

Think about what she's afraid of: a supplier who's too intrusive, who misses the quiet moments, who makes the day feel like a production. Speak to those fears directly in your content — reassure her without ever naming them.

"She's going to a lot of weddings right now. She's watching what her friends get and making mental notes. If you worked at a wedding in her circle and they loved you — she already trusts you before she ever contacts you."

Insight 3
Be on Instagram — at the right time

Amy is active on Instagram on Friday evenings and Saturdays. She browses Pinterest for wedding inspiration. She barely uses TikTok and only dips into Facebook occasionally. This tells you where to spend your energy — and when to post for maximum reach with her specifically.

Your Social Media Planner — the next tool in this workspace — will use your profile to build a posting strategy built around Amy and your top venues.

Insight 4
Your enquiry response is your first impression

Amy is organised and detail-oriented. She sends a considered enquiry and expects a considered reply. A slow, generic, copy-paste response tells her everything she needs to know — and not in a good way. A fast, warm, personalised reply that mentions her venue? That puts you ahead of 90% of your competition immediately.

This is exactly what your Enquiry Response tool is built for. The two tools are designed to work together.

Three Things You Can Do This Week

1. List your top 5 venues. These become the anchor of your content strategy. Every piece of content you create should connect back to one of them.

2. Look at your last 5 Instagram posts. Do they feel like they're speaking to Amy? Are they tagged at venues? Do the captions describe moments and feelings, or just facts?

3. Open your Enquiry Response tool. Read your First Reply — does it sound like it was written for her specifically, or for everyone?
